Objectives

The density adaptive ramp metering algorithm has two main objectives:

  1. Delay the onset of mainline congestion for as long as possible. This is achieved by restricting metering rates when density on the mainline approaches a critical level.
  2. Effectively manage the queue at ramp meters. Wait times should rarely be longer than a configurable limit. Release rates should be adjusted gradually. Also, the queue should not back up onto cross streets.

Bottleneck Identification

At each time interval, all mainline stations on a freeway corridor are checked for bottlenecks. Candidates are selected based on station density.

Candidate Bottlenecks

A mainline station is a candidate bottleneck if one of the following conditions are met:

  • Station density is above bottleneck density for each interval in 90 seconds.
  • Station density is above bottleneck density, AND
    • Station was a bottleneck in previous interval, OR
    • Station density is trending higher for each interval in 90 seconds.

Metering Rates

The ramp queue condition and mainline segment density are used to calculate metering rates. The queue condition imposes minimum and maximum rate limits. Mainline segment density will cause the metering rate to be adjusted within those limits.

Queueing

A queue is formed at a ramp meter when the input (demand) is greater than the output (passage). The total demand and passage counts since the start of metering are compared. At any point in time, the difference between these counts is the current queue length (in vehicles). Also, the wait time is estimated by comparing the current passage count with demand counts from previous time intervals. The vehicle at the head of the queue started waiting when demand was equal to the current passage count.

Flow Rate

The slope of accumulated vehicle counts through time is the flow rate. Typically, it is expressed in vehicles per hour. For practical metering on two-lane alternate-release meters, flow can range from 240 to 1700 (about 4 to 28 vehicles per minute).

Passage (Output)

The passage detector is used to estimate the output of the queue. Unfortunately, vehicle detection systems are not 100% accurate. For improved accuracy, the accumulated passage and demand values are reset to zero when there is no queue.

Demand (Input)

The demand at a ramp meter is estimated using ramp queue detection. A cumulative demand count is taken from the time when metering begins. This demand is treated as the queue input.

If the queue detector is not counting, demand is set to a fixed target value — specific to one ramp meter and period (AM or PM). It is based on historical demand at the ramp.

If the queue backs up over the detector, the occupancy will jump to greater than 25%. When this happens, the detector may undercount vehicles. To correct this, the estimated queue demand is increased gradually until queue length is equal to maximum ramp storage.

The available storage is maximum storage minus queue length. High occupancy duration is the time that occupancy was greater than 25%. Queue overflow ratio is 2 times the high occupancy duration, divided by maximum wait time. The storage demand adjustment is available storage times queue overflow ratio.

Rate Limits

The metering rate is limited to a range between the minimum and maximum rates. These rates are determined by the state of the ramp queue.

Maximum Rate

The maximum metering rate is 130% of the target demand for the previous 5 minutes. This is intended to prevent the meter from releasing all vehicles in a queue too quickly. The maximum rate may be adjusted to match the minimum rate if that is higher.

Minimum Rate

When the passage detector malfunctions, the minimum rate is equal to the average demand rate for the previous 5 minutes. Otherwise, it is the highest of the following 3 limits:

  • Target minimum limit (70% of target demand for previous 5 minutes)
  • Queue wait limit
  • Queue storage limit

Queue Wait Limit

The purpose of the queue wait limit is to prevent vehicles from waiting in the queue for longer than the maximum wait time. A wait limit is calculated for each future interval until the target wait time (75% of maximum wait time). This is done by subtracting the target wait time from each future interval time and checking the accumulated demand at those times. That demand is used to calculate a rate limit from the current accumulated passage. The queue wait limit is the highest of the limits for these intervals.


When the queue detector is malfunctioning, the target value for the current metering period (AM or PM) is used for demand. This makes the queue wait limit behave as if the demand is high at all times. While not optimal, it allows short queues to be held to break up platoons of vehicles.


Queue Storage Limit

The purpose of the queue storage limit is to prevent the queue from becoming more than 75% full. First, the target storage (75% of maximum storage) is subtracted from the accumulated demand. Then, this value is projected into the future to the target wait time (75% of maximum wait time), using the demand rate from the previous 5 minutes. The queue storage limit is the rate from the current accumulated passage to that projected target storage (in the target wait time).


When the queue detector is malfunctioning, the target value for the current metering period (AM or PM) is used for demand. This makes the queue storage limit behave as if the demand is high at all times. While not optimal, it allows short queues to be held to break up platoons of vehicles.


Rate Calculation

The metering rate is calculated iteratively, based on the metering rate from the previous 30 seconds. If the segment density is below the desired density, the rate will increase. Likewise, if segment density is higher than desired, the rate will decrease. When the meter is first turned on, the passage rate averaged over the last 90 seconds is used in place of the previous metering rate.

The previous metering rate is first constrained within the bounds of the new minimum and maximum rates. The new rate is calculated using linear interpolation from the previous rate and density. With segment density between zero and desired density, the rate is interpolated between the maximum rate and the previous rate. With segment density between desired and jam density, the rate is interpolated between the previous rate and the minimum rate.

Metering Period

A metering period begins and ends at specific times during the day. Within the metering period, a meter can be in one of 5 phases: not started, early metering, metering, flushing or stopped. When transition conditions are met, the meter moves to the next phase. If all meters on a corridor are in the stopped phase, the metering period ends.

Not Started Phase

Ramp meters begin each metering period in this phase. The meter will not cycle until the phase is changed. There are two conditions which cause the meter to transition to a different phase. If segment density was higher than bottleneck density for each interval in 90 seconds, the phase will change to early metering. Otherwise, when only 30 minutes remain in the metering period, the phase will change to stopped.

Early Metering Phase

In this phase, a ramp meter will cycle at the selected metering rate. There are three conditions which can cause the meter to transition to the metering phase:

  • Segment density (15 minute average) was trending lower for each interval in 5 minutes.
  • Segment density (1 minute average) was lower than 65% of critical density.
  • Only 30 minutes remain in the metering period.

Metering Phase

In this phase, a ramp meter will cycle at the selected metering rate. The following conditions will cause the meter to transition to the flushing phase:

  • Segment density was below desired density and segment had no bottleneck, for every interval in 5 minutes.
  • Only 2 minutes remain in the metering period.

Flushing Phase

In this phase, a ramp meter will cycle at the maximum metering rate. The following conditions will cause the meter to transition to the stopped phase:

  • The queue is estimated to be empty for 1 minute.
  • The metering period ends.

Stopped Phase

In this phase, a ramp meter will not cycle. There are two conditions which can cause the meter to transition back to the metering phase:

  • Passage flow rate was greater than 80% of selected metering rate for each interval in 5 minutes.
  • Segment density was higher than bottleneck density for each interval in 5 minutes.

Definitions

Bottleneck Density
Density threshold for identifying mainline station bottlenecks (30 vehicles per lane-mile)
Critical Density
Density threshold for identifying high probability of mainline flow breakdown (37 vehicles per lane-mile)
Density
Vehicles per lane-mile
Desired Density
Density threshold for determining whether metering is necessary (80% of critical density, or 29.6 vehicles per lane-mile)
Interval Density
Average of station samples during one 60 second interval
Jam Density
Density of completely jammed mainline (180 vehicles per lane-mile)
Meter Segment
A contiguous series of stations associated with a ramp meter. It begins at the station just upstream of the meter. The downstream end is the first bottleneck within 3 miles (or the last station within 3 miles if no bottleneck exists).
Segment Density
Each consecutive pair of stations is divided into 3 equal links. The first and last links are assigned density from the station adjacent to them. The middle link is assigned a density which is an average of the two stations. The segment density is an average of all links in the segment, weighted by length.
Station Density
Average of all valid lanes in a station for one sample period
